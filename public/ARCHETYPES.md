# The 12 Jungian Archetypes

*A reference for dream analysts and journalers working within the tradition of analytical psychology.*

---

## What Are Archetypes?

Carl Gustav Jung proposed that beneath the personal unconscious lies a deeper layer he called the **collective unconscious** — a reservoir of inherited psychic structures shared by all of humanity. Within this layer live the **archetypes**: universal, primordial patterns of experience that appear across all cultures, mythologies, and dream lives.

Archetypes are not images themselves but rather *tendencies* — innate frameworks that give shape to experience. They manifest in dreams as figures, landscapes, objects, and situations that carry a numinous, charged quality. Working with archetypes in dreams is working with the fundamental grammar of the psyche.

---

## The 12 Primary Archetypes

### 1. The Self - gold
The central archetype — the totality of the psyche, both conscious and unconscious. The Self represents wholeness and the goal of individuation. In dreams it appears as a **divine figure, mandala, wise elder, or radiant child**. When the Self appears, the dream often carries an overwhelming sense of significance or holiness.

*Dream indicators:* mandalas, circles, quaternity (four-fold) symbols, luminous figures, encounters with gods or the cosmos.

---

### 2. The Shadow - black
The Shadow is everything in the personality that the ego has refused to acknowledge — not purely negative, but *unlived*. It carries both repressed darkness and unrealised potential. In dreams, the Shadow typically appears as a **figure of the same sex as the dreamer**, often menacing, inferior, or morally troubling.

Integrating the Shadow means owning what we have projected onto others. Persistent villain figures, embarrassing doubles, or being chased by an unknown assailant are classic Shadow manifestations.

*Dream indicators:* dark pursuer, sinister double, criminal or outcast figure, trickster energy.

---

### 3. The Anima (in men) / Animus (in women) - magenta
The **Anima** is the inner feminine in a man's psyche; the **Animus** is the inner masculine in a woman's psyche. These are the soul figures — bridges to the unconscious — and they appear in dreams as figures of the opposite sex who are strangely familiar, numinous, captivating, or threatening.

The Anima has four developmental levels: Eve (biological woman), Helen (romantic ideal), Mary (spiritual feminine), Sophia (wisdom). The Animus similarly evolves from brute strength toward spiritual logos.

*Dream indicators:* recurring opposite-sex figures (lover, guide, witch, enchantress, hero, sage), relationships that feel fated or uncanny.

---

### 4. The Persona - red
The Persona is the mask we wear for the world — the social identity constructed for public consumption. In dreams, the Persona appears when our identity is under examination. Dreams of **losing one's clothes, wearing a costume, or being exposed** often signal a Persona crisis — the gap between public mask and authentic self.

*Dream indicators:* clothes falling off, wearing a mask or uniform, social embarrassment, performing for an audience.

---

### 5. The Hero - silver
The Hero is the archetype of the ego's struggle against unconscious forces and external obstacles. It represents the developmental impulse — leaving home, confronting the monster, returning transformed. In dreams, the Hero figure undertakes quests, slays dragons, and rescues captives.

The Hero's journey is the journey of individuation itself. But the Hero can become problematic when it over-inflates the ego — the Hero who refuses to admit defeat is approaching the Inflation archetype.

*Dream indicators:* quests, battles with monsters, rescuing others, overcoming impossible obstacles.

---

### 6. The Great Mother6y6 - pink
The Great Mother is the feminine principle of nourishment, fertility, transformation, and devouring darkness. She appears as the protective mother, the nurturing earth, but also as the devouring witch, the terrible mother, the sea that drowns. Nature, caves, the ocean, and round containers are her symbols.

*Dream indicators:* mother figures (benevolent or terrifying), earth, ocean, caves, cauldrons, gardens, ancient women.

---

### 7. The Wise Old Man (Senex) - royal blue
The Wise Old Man is the archetype of meaning, spirit, and depth — the inner guide who appears in dreams as a **sage, guru, prophet, or elder** figure bearing counsel. He often appears at moments of crisis to offer direction, though his advice may be cryptic.

His shadow is the Senex as rigid, judgmental tyrant — the old king who will not relinquish power, the father who crushes the son.

*Dream indicators:* aged guide, hermit, prophet, wizard, teacher, physician, grandfather figure.

---

### 8. The Trickster - orange
The Trickster is the archetype of disruption, transformation through chaos, and the reversal of order. He overturns hierarchies, exposes the absurdity of convention, and — through apparent destruction — creates space for new forms. In dreams, the Trickster appears as a **clown, rogue, shapeshifter, or mischievous figure** who refuses to be pinned down.

The Trickster serves individuation by undermining the ego's rigid plans. Dreams with absurdist humour, sudden reversals, or impossible transformations often carry Trickster energy.

*Dream indicators:* clown, fool, shapeshifter, joker, animal that speaks, impossible physical comedy.

---

### 9. The Child (Puer Aeternus / Puella Aeterna) - baby blue
The Divine Child is the archetype of new beginnings, potentiality, and the future. It appears in dreams as a **miraculous infant, a special child, or the golden child** — often in need of protection. The Child represents the Self in its nascent form and signals the emergence of a new psychological possibility.

Its shadow is the Puer Aeternus (eternal boy) — the man who refuses to grow up, who lives in perpetual potential without commitment.

*Dream indicators:* miraculous or endangered infant, a gifted child, a child who should not exist, rebirth symbols.

---

### 10. The Maiden / Kore - light green 
The Kore (maiden) is the archetype of innocence, receptivity, and transformation through descent. Like Persephone's journey to the underworld, the Kore figure in dreams often appears at the threshold of a major psychological initiation — a descent that will eventually bring new life.

She is related to the Anima but represents the specifically *unawakened* feminine — the soul not yet fully claimed.

*Dream indicators:* young innocent woman in danger, a girl in an unknown territory, an abduction theme, a figure who must pass through darkness.

---

### 11. The Father - dark green 
The Father archetype structures reality, establishes law, and sets the world in order. He is the principle of logos — differentiation, language, and authority. In dreams, he appears as a **king, judge, father, authority figure, or God** who both empowers and constrains.

The negative Father is the tyrant who prevents growth. The positive Father guides initiation.

*Dream indicators:* authority figures, kings, judges, God, law, institutional structures, the sky and sun (as paternal symbols).

---

### 12. The Lover - dark red
The Lover is the archetype of relationship, connection, beauty, and passion. He or she is not merely romantic — this archetype governs the *capacity for deep connection* to people, art, nature, and meaning itself. In dreams, Lover energy appears as erotic encounters, passionate longings, beauty that arrests attention, or deep relational encounters.

When the Lover is wounded, dreams fill with images of isolation, rejection, or loveless landscapes.

*Dream indicators:* romantic encounters, intense beauty, passionate creative work, union with another, erotic imagery (often symbolic).

## Three Resources for Deeper Study

### 1. *The Archetypes and the Collective Unconscious* — C.G. Jung (Collected Works, Vol. 9, Part I)
The foundational text. Jung's own essays on the specific archetypes — Shadow, Anima/Animus, the Self, the Wise Old Man, the Child — read directly from clinical cases and amplification of imagery. Demanding but irreplaceable. Available through Princeton University Press.

### 2. *King, Warrior, Magician, Lover: Rediscovering the Archetypes of the Mature Masculine* — Robert Moore & Douglas Gillette (1990)
A highly accessible synthesis of Jungian archetypal psychology applied to masculine development. Moore and Gillette map four primary archetypes of mature manhood and their shadow forms. Excellent for understanding how archetypes function in daily life and relationships, not only in dreams. Available through HarperCollins.

### 3. *Archetypal Psychology: A Brief Account* — James Hillman (1983, Spring Publications)
Hillman's elegant manifesto for a post-Jungian "archetypal psychology" that resists reducing dream figures to symbols and insists on letting images speak for themselves. Shorter and more poetic than Jung's collected works, this is an ideal entry point for analysts who want a contemporary, phenomenological approach. Available through Spring Publications.
